Zoroastrianism

Zoroastrianism is one of the worldâ€™s oldest religions, founded in ancient Persia by the Prophet Zoroaster. Unlike the Abrahamic religions, it began before Prophet Abraham was born. Zoroastrianism teaches that life is a struggle between good and evil. The good god is Ahura Mazda, who represents light, truth, and goodness. The evil spirit is Angra Mainyu, who represents darkness, lies, and destruction. Their holy book is the Avesta, which contains prayers, hymns, and teachings. Zoroastrianism stresses the importance of choosing good thoughts, good words, and good deeds. Fire is an important symbol, representing purity and the presence of Ahura Mazda. Worship often takes place in fire temples, where a sacred flame is kept burning. Zoroastrians believe in the afterlife, where souls are judged based on their actions. If good outweighs evil, the soul goes to heaven; if not, it suffers punishment. Zoroastrianism also influenced later religions, including Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, with ideas of heaven, hell, and judgment. Though it once was a major world religion, its followers today are much fewer. Festivals such as Nowruz and the Persian New Year are still celebrated. Overall, Zoroastrianism focuses on the struggle between good and evil, the worship of Ahura Mazda, and living a righteous life.
